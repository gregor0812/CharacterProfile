Nobody returns home the same person they were that left the door. For those who venture to the battlefield, coming home is a goal of its own. War is exercised for a myriad of reasons that concern only the dead. It has a way of obscuring humanity like a dreadful fog that turns lives into statistics. For the soldier himself, individuality makes way for instinct and survival. But the Faustian exchange for the primordial instinct that keeps people alive was unwilling to let go one latched on.

Civilizations across the continents have their own name for the phenomenon. The cauterized scars of shaken souls who sold themselves to survive the onslaught. The ever-present guilt of surviving where betters had not. It was not the warcries of the clattering of steel that plagued Jia; to him, his demons were created to keep him on guard during the night that turned to malevolent spirits that denied rest.

Like a third eye that could no longer close demanding vigilance against danger, Jia found himself wanting when he cast away his weapon. The more he tried, the more insurmountable it was to reassert his real self.

Jia Zhang did not become an adventurer because of a sense of duty.
Jia Zhang became an adventurer because of his inability to let go.

First Sights

Hailing from a life of labour on the steppes of Aldenard, Jia possesses a brawny physique tempered by martial experience. Like his imposing kin, he reaches an impressive height of around seven feet.
Though it is not his build that oft is the cause of mistaken belligerence. Jia’s verdant green eyes were ringed with a rich golden hue that made his expression hard to read and easy to interpret as enviable disdain. The Au’ra was easily slotted in the category of plentiful mercenaries that seek to gorge upon the conflict-stricken lands of Eorzea.

Scars

Jia’s skin is slightly tanned and riddled with alabaster scars. Most of these are small and unremarkable, notching themselves along his hand, forearms and legs. A select few, however, are more than a couple of inches long, the most noticeable being the six-inch scar running from the top of his ear to the beginning of his chin, chipping into his scales, and the longest being a foot long marking across his upper back.

First Impression

The first thing that stood out about Jia’s regalia was not necessarily his attire but rather the Gunblade on his back that was reminiscent of Garlean make. Jia’s garb is composed of flowing fabric that preferred form over function. Dressed in surcoats with loose pieces of metal protection offering little protection in a fight. The textiles themselves were of luxurious material and expressed wealth. To those less easily fooled by pretty metals and cloth, Jia’s attire was a poor man’s idea of wealth and oft is considered gaudy by those of true upper class.
